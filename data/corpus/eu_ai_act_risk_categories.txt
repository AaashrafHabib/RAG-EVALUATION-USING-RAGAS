The EU AI Act establishes four categories of risk for AI systems:

1. UNACCEPTABLE RISK (Prohibited AI Practices - Article 5):
AI systems that pose a clear threat to safety, livelihoods, and rights of people are banned entirely. These include:
- Social scoring systems by public authorities
- Real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (with limited exceptions for law enforcement)
- AI systems that deploy subliminal, manipulative, or deceptive techniques to distort behavior
- AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities of specific groups (age, disability, social situation)
- Predictive policing based solely on profiling or personality traits
- Emotion recognition systems in workplace and educational settings
- Untargeted scraping of facial images from the internet or CCTV to build facial recognition databases

2. HIGH RISK (Article 6 and Annex III):
AI systems that pose significant risks to health, safety, or fundamental rights. These must meet strict requirements before being placed on the market. Examples include:
- Biometric identification and categorization systems
- AI used in critical infrastructure management (water, gas, electricity, transport)
- AI systems used in education for determining access or assessing students
- AI used in employment (recruitment, task allocation, performance monitoring)
- AI for access to essential services (credit scoring, insurance pricing)
- AI used in law enforcement (evidence evaluation, recidivism prediction)
- AI used in migration and border control
- AI used in administration of justice and democratic processes

3. LIMITED RISK (Transparency obligations):
AI systems with specific transparency requirements. Users must be informed they are interacting with AI. This includes:
- Chatbots and conversational AI systems
- AI systems that generate deepfakes or synthetic content
- Emotion recognition systems (where not prohibited)
- Biometric categorization systems (where not prohibited)

4. MINIMAL RISK:
All other AI systems that pose minimal or no risk to citizens' rights or safety. These can be developed and used freely, subject only to existing legislation. Examples include AI-enabled video games, spam filters, and inventory management systems.
